The Foundational Concepts

This model is built on a few core "what if" propositions. The rest of the theory is an exploration of the logical consequences of accepting them as true.

I. Relative Temporo-Axial Disagreement

The core proposition is that our perception of reality is essentially inverted. We do not move through a three dimensional space container while time ticks by. Instead, each fundamental particle of matter is in constant, invariant motion at speed c along a unique temporal axis. This true motion is inward (the future), a direction we cannot perceive. Space is the outward (past) propagation of information between each distinct inertial frame across relative time. Systems of matter are collections of these temporal axes influenced by interactions which are shaped by the relative geometry of their disagreement on the direction of time. The fundamental constituents of systems bound by these relative geometric interactions each invariably continue along their temporal axes inward at c, and thus the relative information of the past is in flux outward from each of them toward all the rest. Since they are bound by interaction at unique temporal angles relative to each other, and from the perspective of any such subject frame time must always progress at c, in every relative frame time must progress at less than c based on how severely the angle of its temporal axis diverges from the unadulterated angle of the subject frame in perspective.

II. The Temporo-Gyroscopic Model

This model is a mechanism used to describe the point of inversion between the temporal information we perceive directly and the temporal information we perceive only indirectly. Each fundamental particle is a temporal gyroscope with innately distinct inertial frame which moves inward along a linear axis through time at c. It possesses three spinning axes in addition to this linear in/out temporal axis. The primary spinning axis is distinct to each particle in that its mass is proportional to the energy of this spin. The remaining two axes of spin are kinetic in nature, such that from the perspective of the subject frame in the absence of external forces they remain at rest. External forces may apply energy to these kinetic axes, causing them to spin and generate an angular momentum vector which influences temporo-axial alignment. What we perceive as motion in space (v_s) is merely the apparent precession of a temporal axis which is not the subject of the frame.

III. The Thermodynamic Imperative (The "Flux")

This is the "why" that drives all motion. The universe is a single "Thermodynamic Field" (TDF) in a state of disequilibrium. All forces and interactions are an expression of a single, universal imperative—the "Flux"—for this field to resolve its disequilibrium and return to an equilibrium state. This is the true engine of the universe and the fundamental "arrow of time."

IV. Emergent Reality (Space, Force, and Matter)

From these concepts, it follows that the things we consider fundamental are actually emergent properties:

  • Space is the perceived, relational, "outward" (past) geometry of all temporal axes.
  • Force is the geometric expression of the "Flux," the "torque" that tilts temporal gyroscopes to resolve thermodynamic disequilibrium.
  • Matter is a collection of these gyroscopes, bound into structures by the influence of their mutual "temporal disagreement."
  • Temperature is the measure of the *magnitude* of this internal, geometric disagreement within a bound structure.

V. The Geometric Bridge (The Angle α)

This model is not just a philosophical one. It is anchored to testable reality by a single, geometric bridge. The "disagreement angle" (α) between your temporal axis and another's is the key. This angle directly partitions your invariant motion c into its perceived spatial (v_s) and temporal (v_t) components. This geometry *derives* the mathematics of Special Relativity, rather than just postulating them, starting with its core relationship:

sin(α) = v_s / c

From this single, geometric link, the entire framework of relativity (the Lorentz factor, time dilation, etc.) can be derived as a matter of simple trigonometry.
